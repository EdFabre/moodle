One-Way Analysis of Variance (continued) View and evaluate the results.

In this task, you view and evaluate the results of the ANOVA analysis.

First, you should evaluate how well the ANOVA assumptions were met. Look at the tests for equal variances near the middle of the results.

SAS Enterprise Guide produces several tests for homogeneity of variance, including Levene’s test, Brown and Forsythe’s test, and Bartlett’s test. These tests each use different methods of assessing whether the samples have sufficiently different variability to reject the null hypothesis of equal variability.

All the tests have large p-values, suggesting that there is insufficient evidence to conclude that the variances are unequal. In other words, the assumption of equal variances has been sufficiently met.



Click to view the full window.


Scroll back to the top of the output window. The ANOVA table shows a statistically significant F-statistic. There is sufficient evidence to reject the null hypothesis of equal group means. However, you do not know where the mean differences are until you look over the means comparisons tests.



Click to view the full window.


Scroll to the Bonferroni t-test report. Groups with the same letter are not significantly different from one another. From this report, you can conclude that the means for Graystone and Consolidated are significantly different.



Click to view the full window.


Scroll down to the Tukey's Studentized Range (HSD) report, which is a different method of comparing means than the Bonferroni, as evidenced by the Least Significant Difference for type of test. Tukey’s HSD calculates a value (the Honestly Significant Difference) that reflects the difference between group means adjusted for the desired experimentwise alpha. The Tukey report provides the same general conclusion as the Bonferroni t-test report. The means for Graystone and Consolidated are significantly different.

Control Charts for Measurement Data

Overview

There is variability in every product; no two products are ever identical. One of the primary goals of quality improvement is to understand and reduce process variability. In order to assess variability, you collect samples at rational subgroups (usually time periods). The subgroups are defined such that

  • between-sample variability identifies potential problems
  • within-sample variability corresponds to the variability inherent in the process.

Control charts such as XBar and S charts can be used to monitor sample variability. These charts can reveal

  • a cyclic pattern in sample means, such as seasonal effects
  • a pattern suggesting that your samples come from two different processes, such as two work shifts
  • a shift in the process at a point in time, perhaps when a new element was added to the process or servicing was conducted
  • a gradual increase or decrease in the process level, such as deterioration of mechanical parts.

In addition to revealing patterns between samples, control charts can also help you detect variation within samples. For example, S and R charts allow you to identify changes in within-sample variability at a particular point in time.

This example uses a data table that includes information about the temperature of frozen desserts when they arrive at a store on a refrigerated delivery truck. The example shows how to navigate and generate control charts for the mean, the range, and the standard deviation, and how to apply tests for special causes to the control charts.

The IceCream data set has the following variables:


Variable Description
Temperature temperature of desserts in degrees F
Sampleid a label for the sample, numbered in chronological order
Sequence indicates whether each dessert was the first, second, third, fourth, or fifth unit to be measured within the sample
